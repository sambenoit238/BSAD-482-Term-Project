# Archetype Analysis #

## Archetype Mapping ##

One of the archetype structures that fits this decision is shifting the burden which is defined as a quick fix that undermines the capacity for fundemental solutions. Through this archetype, we know the problem is that people with disabilities have a lower unemployment rate than those without disabilities. To fix this issue quickly, the federal government could establish employment programs that incentivise organizations to hire more people with disabilities. While this might improve the employment levels for people with disabilities momenetarily as workplaces have money to make their offices a little more accessible for the short term, the problem itself isn't fixed entirely. Because of this quick fix, there might be some unintended consequences. These might include: the creation of temporary accomodations that make it difficult for people with disabilities to perform their jobs effectively, employees feel people with disabilities are hired only for DEIA reasons and not because of their skills or qualifications and employers only hiring those with disabilities if incentives are involved. To avoid these unintended consequences, employers could still be provided with an incentive to make their workplaces more accessible by the federal government, but also take courses or learn from those with disabilities why it's important to hire those with disabilities and the importance of making their workplaces more accessible to all. In short, because employers rely on incentives from the government to encourage hiring without having the education on inclusive practices, the system depends on these quick fixes. As a result people with disabilities may be hired for DEIA reasons rather than because of their skills and qualifications and they may not receive the accomodations needed to work effectively.

# Scenerio Narratives #

## Status Quo ##

If the federal government were not to take any action and just follow the “status quo”, the employment rate for people with disabilities would still be much lower than the employment rate for those without disabilities like the data shows today. As of right now and as shown in the quick fix loop, any incentives that the government has in place for employers to hire those with disabilities still increases the disability employment rate, which then increases the employers dependence on using government incentives to hire those with disabilities, increasing the number and amount of government incentives given out. While this might be working to increase the number of people with disabilities who are employed, there are 2 consequences that connect to the employer’s dependence on government incentives to hire those with disabilities. The first consequence is that by depending on government incentives, the quality of accommodations and overall workplace accessibility decreases as employers attempt to find the cheapest and most temporary ways to make their workplaces accessible for the people they are hiring. The second consequence to depending on government incentives to hire those with disabilities is that when dependence increases, employers knowledge and understanding of inclusive hiring decreases, causing the employer to hire people with disabilities for DEIA purposes without understanding why DEIA in the workplace matters because it allows for more than one idea or belief to be shared.

## Intervention A ##

If the government chooses to follow Intervention A, they will follow the fundamental loop in the archetype diagram. By providing classes and/or resources to employers that are about why hiring people with disabilities and other minorities in the DEIA categories is important in workplaces, that are designed by people with disabilities and other minorities, employers would have a much better understanding about hiring diversely so that they don’t just “check off a box” but also so they have more diverse opinions and ideas to drive their mission and vision, the government will see an increase in the disability employment rate. When the disability employment rate rises, so will the quality of accommodation and workplace accessibility. Unlike in the status quo, where accommodation and workplace accessibility would most likely be temporary, in Intervention A, they would most likely be more permanent, because employers are listening to those with disabilities rather than the government who is giving them money to make these changes. While this also causes the employment rate to rise on its own, it would also increase the employer knowledge and understanding of inclusive hiring as no disability is the same. While this intervention may be more expensive on the employer side, as there is little to no government incentive being provided, it still makes a major difference to those who live not only with disabilities, but those who identify as other minorities as well as everyone feels more included in the workplace. 

## Intervention B ##

If the government were to follow Intervention B, they would still be following the fundamental loop in the diagram, but with the inclusion of government incentives for employers. The Government would still provide training and resources to employers that were created by people with disabilities (and other minorities who have a low employment rate), about why hiring inclusively doesn’t just allow employers to “tick a box” but also have a much more diverse workplace with more opinions and ideas that drive the mission and vision of the organization. While this increases employer knowledge and understanding about inclusive hiring it would also increase the disability employment rate. Because organizations have the knowledge as to why it’s important to hire those with disabilities, they are more likely to employ more people with disabilities, raising the disability employment rate and in return the government could increase incentives to employers that would help them make their workplaces more accessible, thus increasing the quality of accommodations and workplace accessibility. The increase in quality of accommodations and workplace accessibility would then not only increase the disability employment rate, as more employers are prepared to have people with disabilities in their workforce, but also the knowledge of inclusive hiring, as they now have voices inside the organization who can advocate for better accessibility or more employees who look like them.
